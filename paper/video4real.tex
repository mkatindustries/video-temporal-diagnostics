\begin{abstract}
Scalable video retrieval favors global descriptors that pool frame features and are largely
insensitive to motion direction: two traversals of the same road in opposite directions, or left
versus right turns at the same intersection, can receive similar scores. Sequence comparators such
as dynamic time warping (DTW) over per-frame features \emph{detect conditional maneuver
discrimination}---telling such cases apart \emph{within} a known location. We ask whether that
advantage transfers to end-to-end retrieval, and find a consistent reversal on two driving
datasets. V-JEPA~2 encoder-sequence DTW clearly beats a bag-of-tokens (BoT) cosine baseline on
conditional pooled-pair discrimination (Honda HDD $0.942$ vs.\ $0.825$ AP; nuScenes $0.867$ vs.\
$0.791$) yet \emph{underperforms} it on global query-wise retrieval (HDD $0.177$ vs.\ $0.256$ mAP;
nuScenes $0.141$ vs.\ $0.315$). A BoT$\to$DTW cascade lowers AP at every evaluated $k$, and a
leakage-safe held-out linear fusion shows no detected gain over BoT on HDD ($+0.001$,
$95\%$ CI $[-0.003, 0.004]$; $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$) and collapses exactly onto BoT on nuScenes
($\alpha^\star{=}1.0$). A second, orthogonal failure concerns the interface: temporal evidence can
be present in multimodal LLMs (integrity-framed forward/reverse classification reaches
$0.845$--$0.879$ balanced accuracy) yet direct direction prompts sit near chance and pooled readouts
are order-invariant, so standard prompting and pooling fail to expose it. Temporal sensitivity is
thus necessary for some motion queries but---for these datasets, scores, and a linear fusion
rule---not sufficient for retrieval, and upgrading to a multimodal model does not resolve the
readout problem. We release the diagnostics and evaluation protocol.
\keywords{Video retrieval \and Temporal sensitivity \and Evaluation \and Place recognition}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Video-retrieval systems that scale to large galleries typically represent a clip by a single pooled
descriptor and rank by cosine similarity. Such descriptors are, by construction, largely
insensitive to frame \emph{order}: reversing a clip or swapping a left turn for a right turn at the
same intersection changes little in a mean-pooled embedding. A natural remedy compares per-frame
feature \emph{sequences} with an order-aware comparator such as dynamic time
warping (DTW)~\cite{sakoe1978dtw}, which is markedly stronger on conditional discrimination.

We test whether that conditional strength matters for practice, and report two connected failures.
\textbf{(i) Retrieval.} On a directed full-gallery protocol over Honda HDD~\cite{honda_hdd} and
nuScenes~\cite{nuscenes}, the order-aware comparator that wins \emph{conditionally} loses
\emph{globally}; a fixed BoT$\to$DTW cascade degrades accuracy at every operating point; and a
cross-validated linear fusion of the appearance and temporal scores assigns essentially all weight
to appearance. \textbf{(ii) Interface.} Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) do carry recoverable temporal
evidence, but standard direct prompts and pooled embeddings do not expose it, so ``use a bigger
multimodal model'' does not resolve the problem either. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A directed, query-wise full-gallery retrieval protocol with intersection-cluster bootstrap
  uncertainty, distinguishing \emph{conditional pooled-pair} discrimination from \emph{global}
  retrieval, evaluated on two driving datasets.
  \item Evidence that a strong conditional temporal comparator reverses under global retrieval, and
  that neither a cascade nor a leakage-safe held-out linear fusion recovers the gap.
  \item A scoped MLLM readout analysis showing prompt- and pooling-dependent exposure of temporal
  evidence, and that a temporally adapted MLLM with single-vector cosine remains weak on driving
  conditional discrimination.
\end{itemize}
The claims are deliberately scoped to these datasets, the representations and comparators tested,
and a linear fusion protocol; they are not a statement about video models in general.

\section{Diagnostics and Protocols}
\label{sec:protocols}
\textbf{Representations and comparators.} Pooled, order-invariant baselines---bag of frames (BoF),
Chamfer matching, and a V-JEPA~2~\cite{vjepa2} bag-of-tokens (BoT) cosine descriptor---are compared
against order-aware sequence comparators on per-frame features: V-JEPA~2 encoder-sequence DTW, a
temporal-residual DTW, and a DINOv3~\cite{dinov3} attention-trajectory variant; a symmetric temporal
max-similarity~\cite{kordopatis2019visil} is a further sequence baseline. V-JEPA~2 ingests $64$
frames as $32\times16\times16$ spatiotemporal patches ($D{=}1024$); the encoder sequence
spatially averages patches per temporal position to a $(32, D)$ trajectory, and BoT mean-pools all
patch tokens to one vector.

\textbf{DTW and normalization.} DTW aligns two sequences by a minimum-cost monotonic path that may
stretch but not reverse time. Each sequence and feature dimension is independently min--max
normalized over time; the accumulated path cost is normalized by $T_1{+}T_2$, and similarity is
$\exp(-d_\mathrm{DTW})$. For one distance matrix this transform is monotone, so AP rankings are
invariant to the scale constant while the similarity values are not; scores from different
comparators are therefore not numerically comparable.

\textbf{Segments, clusters, eligibility.} Maneuver segments are grouped into intersections by DBSCAN
on GPS position ($\epsilon{\approx}30$\,m, min.\ samples $2$); an intersection is retained if it
contains both left and right turns. A candidate is \emph{relevant} to a query if it shares the
query's intersection cluster \emph{and} maneuver label; out-of-cluster candidates remain negatives.
A segment is an eligible query if it has at least one relevant gallery item. HDD yields $1{,}687$
segments in $50$ mixed clusters; nuScenes (v1.0-trainval) yields $264$ segments from $50$ clusters,
with $222$ eligible queries drawn from $40$ clusters.

\textbf{Directed full-gallery retrieval and fusion.} Each eligible segment queries all others; we
report query-macro mAP and mean reciprocal rank (MRR). We test (i) a BoT$\to$DTW cascade that
reranks the BoT top-$k$ by DTW, and (ii) a per-query linear fusion
$\alpha\,z(\text{BoT}) + (1{-}\alpha)\,z(-\text{DTW})$ of $z$-scored scores over the valid gallery.
The weight $\alpha$ is chosen over a $21$-point grid on $[0,1]$ by leave-one-cluster-out
cross-validation: for each held-out intersection, $\alpha$ maximizes mean training-query AP on the
other clusters with the held-out cluster removed from both the tuning queries and their galleries;
ties keep a single maximizing endpoint, else the middle maximizing grid point. The selected
$\alpha$ is then applied to the held-out queries against the \emph{full} gallery, so no query
informs its own weight.

\textbf{Uncertainty.} All estimates are query-macro means with $95\%$ intersection-cluster bootstrap
intervals (resampling clusters, $2000$ resamples). We report \emph{marginal} intervals per method
and \emph{paired} difference intervals for named contrasts separately; paired intervals are never
attached to a single method's estimate. We report the bootstrap fraction of resamples favoring one
method as a descriptive quantity, not a significance test.

\textbf{Order-sensitivity motivation.} Scrambling a clip into $K$ near-equal shuffled chunks
($K\!\in\!\{1,\dots,16\}$, ten seeds) on VCDB~\cite{vcdb} (\cref{fig:scramble}) shows that
order-aware representations and comparators respond to scrambling while pooled baselines remain
flat, confirming the comparators carry genuine order information before any retrieval question.

\section{Retrieval: Conditional Gains Reverse Globally}
\label{sec:retrieval}
\textbf{Conditional discrimination favors the sequence comparator.} \cref{tab:reversal} (top) shows
encoder-sequence DTW attains the highest conditional pooled-pair AP on both datasets; the paired
cluster-bootstrap difference over BoT excludes zero on both (HDD $+0.117$, $[0.044, 0.127]$;
nuScenes $+0.075$, $[0.028, 0.128]$).

\textbf{The ranking reverses in global retrieval.} On the directed full-gallery protocol
(\cref{tab:reversal}, middle) the order inverts in both mAP and MRR: BoT reaches mAP $0.256$ / MRR
$0.766$ on HDD and $0.315$ / $0.554$ on nuScenes, while encoder-sequence DTW reaches only
$0.177$ / $0.640$ and $0.141$ / $0.259$. The paired global difference encoder-sequence DTW $-$ BoT
is $-0.175$ $[-0.245, -0.117]$ on nuScenes and \hddGlobalDtwBot{} on HDD. DTW has no notion of
location and promotes temporally similar segments from the wrong intersection once out-of-cluster
negatives are restored.

\textbf{Neither cascade nor held-out fusion recovers the gap.} Reranking the BoT top-$k$ by DTW
lowers AP at every evaluated $k$ on the same candidate set (recall unchanged; \cref{fig:cascade}).
The held-out linear fusion (\cref{fig:loco}) selects $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$ in all $50$ HDD folds and
$\alpha^\star{=}1.0$ in all $40$ nuScenes folds: on HDD the fused mAP shows no detected gain over
BoT ($+0.001$, $[-0.003, 0.004]$), and on nuScenes it collapses exactly onto BoT (difference $0$).

\section{Interface: Temporal Evidence Exists but Readouts Hide It}
\label{sec:mllm}
The retrieval failure above uses self-supervised video features. A natural question is whether
multimodal LLMs, with stronger semantics, expose the temporal signal instead. They do not do so
reliably, for reasons of \emph{prompt} and \emph{readout} rather than absence of evidence
(\cref{fig:vlm}). On EPIC-Kitchens~\cite{epickitchens} forward/reverse classification, direct
direction prompts leave open-weight MLLMs near chance (Gemma-4 $0.538$, LLaVA-Video $0.503$,
Qwen3-VL $0.508$ balanced accuracy), yet an intact-vs-tampered \emph{integrity} prompt on the same
clips reaches $0.845$ (Gemma-4) and $0.879$ (Qwen3-VL); LLaVA-Video degenerates to constant output.
At the embedding level, pooled vision/LLM readouts are order-invariant (reversal score
$s_\mathrm{rev}\!\approx\!0.998$--$1.000$ under cosine), while per-frame sequence readouts respond to
reversal on their own DTW scale ($s_\mathrm{rev}\!\approx\!0.49$--$0.54$).

Crucially, these MLLM readouts are also weak at driving conditional discrimination relative to
V-JEPA~2 (\cref{tab:readout}), and a temporally adapted MLLM does not close the gap: TARA~\cite{tara},
a 7B model trained with chiral negatives, reaches only $0.547$ conditional AP on HDD under
single-vector cosine. We therefore make the narrow claim that upgrading to a multimodal model does
not by itself expose the temporal signal; we do \emph{not} claim MLLMs reproduce the
conditional-to-global retrieval reversal, which would require a full-gallery MLLM retrieval
experiment we do not run here.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Global video descriptors and pooled matching underpin scalable near-duplicate and event
retrieval~\cite{faiss}, while sequence-alignment comparators such as DTW and temporal
max-similarity~\cite{kordopatis2019visil} capture fine-grained order. Our location-vs-order tension
is closely related to \emph{visual place recognition} (VPR), where global aggregation such as
NetVLAD~\cite{netvlad} enables scalable place matching, and \emph{sequence}-based localization such
as SeqSLAM~\cite{seqslam} and learned sequence descriptors~\cite{seqnet} exploit temporal order
along a route. Our result is a caution for that line: an order-aware sequence score that helps
\emph{within} a place can hurt \emph{across} places if it is not itself place-discriminative.
Analyses of temporal understanding in video and multimodal models largely evaluate conditional or
classification settings; recent theory also bounds what a fixed-dimensional embedding ranking can
express~\cite{weller2025limits}, consistent with our finding that a temporal score appended to an
appearance ranking need not improve it.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Conditional maneuver discrimination and global retrieval measure different capabilities. A
comparator can separate left from right turns at a known intersection yet, lacking any location
signal, rank the whole gallery worse than an appearance descriptor; a fixed reranking stage or a
cross-validated linear blend of the two scores does not repair this, because the temporal score
carries no information about \emph{where}. In parallel, multimodal LLMs can hold recoverable
temporal evidence that standard prompts and pooled readouts fail to surface. Recovering global
temporal retrieval therefore requires a temporal representation that is itself
location-discriminative, and an interface that exposes order rather than pooling it away---not
merely a better rule over existing scores or a larger model. These conclusions are scoped to two
driving datasets, the specific representations and comparators evaluated, and a linear fusion
protocol; we release the diagnostics and directed-retrieval/fusion evaluation to support broader
study.
