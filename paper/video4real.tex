\begin{abstract}
Scalable video retrieval favors global descriptors that pool frame features and are largely
insensitive to motion direction: two traversals of the same road in opposite directions, or left
versus right turns at the same intersection, can receive similar scores. Sequence comparators such
as dynamic time warping (DTW) over per-frame features \emph{detect conditional maneuver
discrimination}---telling such cases apart \emph{within} a known location. We ask whether that
advantage transfers across intersections and observe the same failure on two driving
datasets. V-JEPA~2 encoder-sequence DTW clearly beats a bag-of-tokens (BoT) cosine baseline on
conditional pooled-pair discrimination (Honda HDD $0.942$ vs.\ $0.825$ AP; nuScenes $0.867$ vs.\
$0.791$) yet \emph{underperforms} it over the full evaluation gallery (HDD $0.177$ vs.\ $0.256$
mAP; nuScenes $0.141$ vs.\ $0.315$). Temporal-residual DTW, the conditional HDD winner
($0.956$ AP), is weaker still globally ($0.164$ HDD; $0.122$ nuScenes mAP). Across all six top-1 rankings, at least $98.9\%$ of errors
retrieve the wrong intersection rather than the wrong maneuver at the correct one. A
BoT$\to$encoder-sequence-DTW cascade lowers AP at every evaluated $k$, and a
leakage-safe held-out BoT/encoder-sequence fusion shows no detected gain on HDD ($+0.001$,
$95\%$ CI $[-0.003, 0.004]$; $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$) and collapses exactly onto BoT on nuScenes
($\alpha^\star{=}1.0$). A second, orthogonal failure concerns the interface: temporal evidence can
be present in multimodal LLMs (an integrity prompt on forward/reverse clips reaches
$0.845$--$0.879$ balanced accuracy) yet direct direction prompts sit near chance and pooled readouts
are empirically near-invariant, so standard prompting and pooling fail to expose it reliably. For
these datasets, scores, and a linear fusion rule, conditional temporal sensitivity is useful yet
insufficient for global retrieval; upgrading to a multimodal model also does not by itself resolve
the readout problem. We release the diagnostics and evaluation protocol.
\keywords{Video retrieval \and Temporal sensitivity \and Evaluation \and Place recognition}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Video-retrieval systems that scale to large galleries typically represent a clip by a single pooled
descriptor and rank by cosine similarity. Such pooling can be insensitive to order, so reversing a
clip or comparing opposite turns at one intersection can change little. This is exact for pooled
independent-frame features but only empirical for contextual video tokens. A natural remedy compares per-frame
feature \emph{sequences} with an order-aware comparator such as dynamic time
warping (DTW)~\cite{sakoe1978dtw}, which is markedly stronger on conditional discrimination.
Yet its full-gallery failures are overwhelmingly wrong-location retrievals
(\cref{fig:error_composition}).

\begin{figure}[t!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{v4r_error_composition.png}
  \caption{Top-1 outcome composition over the full evaluation gallery. Each retrieved item is
  relevant (same intersection and maneuver), a maneuver error at the correct intersection, or an
  error from a different intersection. This separates conditional maneuver discrimination from
  location discrimination.}
  \label{fig:error_composition}
\end{figure}

We test whether that conditional strength matters for practice, and report two connected failures.
\textbf{(i) Retrieval.} On directed retrieval over the full evaluation galleries of Honda HDD~\cite{honda_hdd} and
nuScenes~\cite{nuscenes}, both tested order-aware comparators lose \emph{globally}, including the
temporal residual that wins conditionally on HDD; a fixed BoT$\to$encoder-sequence-DTW cascade degrades accuracy at every evaluated operating point; and a
cross-validated linear fusion of the BoT and encoder-sequence scores assigns essentially all weight
to appearance. \textbf{(ii) Interface.} Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) do carry recoverable temporal
evidence, but standard direct prompts and pooled embeddings do not expose it, so ``use a bigger
multimodal model'' does not resolve the problem either. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A directed, query-wise evaluation-gallery protocol with intersection-cluster bootstrap
  uncertainty, distinguishing \emph{conditional pooled-pair} discrimination from \emph{global}
  retrieval, evaluated on two driving datasets.
  \item Evidence that both sequence comparators are strong conditionally yet underperform globally,
  and that encoder-sequence reranking and held-out fusion do not recover the gap.
  \item A scoped MLLM readout analysis showing prompt- and pooling-dependent exposure of temporal
  evidence, and that a temporally adapted MLLM with single-vector cosine remains weak on driving
  conditional discrimination.
\end{itemize}
Anonymous code and evaluation artifacts are available online.\footnote{\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/video-temporal-diagnostics-F450}}
Our claims are scoped to these datasets, representations, comparators, and the linear fusion
protocol, not video models in general.

\section{Diagnostics and Protocols}
\label{sec:protocols}
\textbf{Representations and comparators.} V-JEPA~2~\cite{vjepa2} BoT mean-pools contextual tokens
and is empirically near-invariant, unlike exactly invariant pooling of independent-frame features.
We compare it with V-JEPA~2 encoder-sequence DTW and temporal-residual DTW. V-JEPA~2 ingests $64$
frames as $32\times16\times16$ spatiotemporal patches ($D{=}1024$); the encoder sequence
spatially averages patches per temporal position to a $(32, D)$ trajectory, the residual averages
predictor--target differences at each of $16$ target positions, and BoT mean-pools all patch tokens
to one vector.

\textbf{DTW and normalization.} DTW aligns two sequences by a minimum-cost monotonic path that may
stretch but not reverse time. Each sequence and feature dimension is independently min--max
normalized over time; the accumulated path cost is normalized by $T_1{+}T_2$, and similarity is
$\exp(-d_\mathrm{DTW})$. For one distance matrix this transform is monotone, so AP rankings are
invariant to the scale constant while the similarity values are not; scores from different
comparators are therefore not numerically comparable.

\textbf{Segments, clusters, eligibility.} Maneuver segments are grouped into intersections by DBSCAN
on GPS position ($\epsilon{\approx}30$\,m, min.\ samples $2$); an intersection is retained if it
contains both left and right turns. A candidate is \emph{relevant} to a query if it shares the
query's intersection cluster \emph{and} maneuver label; out-of-cluster candidates remain negatives.
A segment is an eligible query if it has at least one relevant gallery item. HDD yields $1{,}687$
segments in the $50$ largest mixed clusters; nuScenes (v1.0-trainval) yields $264$ segments from
the $50$ largest mixed clusters, with $222$ eligible queries drawn from $40$ clusters.

\textbf{Directed retrieval and fusion.} Each eligible segment queries every other retained segment
(the full \emph{evaluation} gallery, not the source dataset). We report query-macro mAP and
mean reciprocal rank (MRR). We test (i) a cascade that reranks the BoT top-$k$ with
encoder-sequence DTW, and (ii) a per-query linear fusion of the same two scores,
$\alpha\,z(\text{BoT}) + (1{-}\alpha)\,z(-d_\mathrm{enc})$, over the valid gallery, where
$d_\mathrm{enc}$ is the encoder-sequence DTW distance.
The weight $\alpha$ is chosen over a $21$-point grid on $[0,1]$ by leave-one-cluster-out
cross-validation: for each held-out intersection, $\alpha$ maximizes mean training-query AP on the
other clusters with the held-out cluster removed from both the tuning queries and their galleries;
ties keep a single maximizing endpoint, else the middle maximizing grid point. The selected
$\alpha$ is then applied to the held-out queries against the \emph{full} gallery, so no query
informs its own weight.

\textbf{Uncertainty.} Conditional results are pooled-pair AP; gallery results are query-macro means.
For both, $95\%$ intervals use $2000$ intersection-cluster bootstrap resamples. We report
\emph{marginal} intervals per method and \emph{paired} difference intervals for named contrasts
separately; paired intervals are never attached to a single method's estimate. Fusion intervals
bootstrap fixed out-of-fold query metrics rather than refitting $\alpha$ in every resample. We do
not interpret the fraction of resamples favoring one method as a $p$-value.

\section{Retrieval: Conditional Gains Reverse Globally}
\label{sec:retrieval}
\textbf{Conditional discrimination favors sequence comparison.} \cref{tab:reversal} (top) shows
temporal-residual DTW has the highest conditional pooled-pair AP on HDD ($0.956$), while
encoder-sequence DTW leads on nuScenes ($0.867$). Paired encoder-sequence gains over BoT exclude
zero: $+0.117$ $[0.044, 0.127]$ (HDD) and $+0.075$ $[0.028, 0.128]$ (nuScenes).

\textbf{The ranking reverses across the evaluation gallery.} Under directed gallery retrieval
(\cref{tab:reversal}, middle) both sequence scores lose in mAP and MRR: BoT reaches mAP $0.256$ /
MRR $0.766$ on HDD and $0.315$ / $0.554$ on nuScenes, while encoder-sequence DTW reaches only
$0.177$ / $0.640$ and $0.141$ / $0.259$. Temporal-residual DTW falls further to $0.164$ / $0.616$
and $0.122$ / $0.231$; its paired mAP deficit to BoT is $-0.091$ $[-0.116, -0.077]$ on HDD and
$-0.193$ $[-0.270, -0.135]$ on nuScenes. The result is consistent with insufficient location
discrimination; we test that explanation by decomposing top-ranked outcomes.

\textbf{Top-1 error decomposition.} \cref{fig:error_composition} separates failures at the
correct intersection from retrievals of the wrong location. Same-intersection/wrong-maneuver
outcomes are at most $0.5\%$ for every top-1 ranking. Encoder-sequence DTW increases wrong-intersection
retrieval over BoT by $+0.142$ $[0.108, 0.187]$ on HDD and $+0.306$ $[0.229, 0.387]$ on
nuScenes; residual DTW increases it by $+0.172$ $[0.137, 0.214]$ and $+0.338$ $[0.256, 0.419]$
(paired cluster bootstrap). Thus the conditional gain does not survive location search:
the global error is overwhelmingly selecting the wrong intersection, not the wrong maneuver at the
right one.

\textbf{Neither encoder-sequence cascade nor held-out fusion recovers the gap.} Reranking the BoT top-$k$ by encoder-sequence DTW
lowers AP at every evaluated $k$ on the same candidate set (recall unchanged; \cref{fig:cascade}).
The held-out BoT/encoder-sequence fusion selects $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$ in all $50$ HDD folds and
$\alpha^\star{=}1.0$ in all $40$ nuScenes folds: on HDD the fused mAP shows no detected gain over
BoT ($+0.001$, $[-0.003, 0.004]$), and on nuScenes it collapses exactly onto BoT (difference $0$).

\section{Interface: Temporal Evidence Exists but Readouts Hide It}
\label{sec:mllm}
The retrieval failure above uses self-supervised video features. A natural question is whether
multimodal LLMs, with stronger semantics, expose the temporal signal instead. They do not do so
reliably, for reasons of \emph{prompt} and \emph{readout} rather than absence of evidence
(\cref{fig:vlm}). On EPIC-Kitchens~\cite{epickitchens} forward/reverse classification, three direct
prompts leave Gemma-4~\cite{gemma4} ($0.538$), LLaVA-Video~\cite{llavavideo} ($0.503$), and
Qwen3-VL~\cite{qwen3vl} ($0.508$) near chance in mean balanced accuracy. Yet an
intact-vs-tampered \emph{integrity} prompt on the same
clips reaches $0.845$ (Gemma-4) and $0.879$ (Qwen3-VL); LLaVA-Video degenerates to constant output.
At the embedding level, pooled vision/LLM readouts change little under reversal (reversal score
$s_\mathrm{rev}\!\approx\!0.998$--$1.000$ under cosine), while per-frame sequence readouts respond to
reversal on their own DTW scale ($s_\mathrm{rev}\!\approx\!0.49$--$0.54$). The Qwen vision values
are approximate because its dynamic-resolution 3D token merge prevents exact frame grouping.

Crucially, these MLLM readouts are also weak at driving conditional discrimination relative to
V-JEPA~2 (\cref{tab:readout}), and a temporally adapted MLLM does not close the gap: TARA~\cite{tara},
a 7B model trained with chiral negatives, reaches only $0.547$ conditional AP on HDD under
single-vector cosine. We therefore make the narrow claim that upgrading to a multimodal model does
not by itself expose the temporal signal; we do \emph{not} claim MLLMs reproduce the
conditional-to-global retrieval reversal, which would require an evaluation-gallery MLLM retrieval
experiment we do not run here.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Global video descriptors and pooled matching underpin scalable near-duplicate and event
retrieval~\cite{faiss}, while sequence-alignment comparators such as DTW, Drop-DTW, and temporal
max-similarity capture fine-grained order~\cite{sakoe1978dtw,dropdtw,kordopatis2019visil}. Our location-vs-order tension
is closely related to \emph{visual place recognition} (VPR), where global aggregation such as
NetVLAD~\cite{netvlad} enables scalable place matching, and \emph{sequence}-based localization such
as SeqSLAM~\cite{seqslam} and learned sequence descriptors~\cite{seqnet} exploit temporal order
along a route. Our result is a caution for that line: an order-aware sequence score that helps
\emph{within} a place can hurt \emph{across} places if it is not itself place-discriminative.
Analyses of temporal understanding in video and multimodal models largely evaluate conditional or
classification settings; recent theory also bounds what a fixed-dimensional embedding ranking can
express~\cite{weller2025limits}, consistent with our finding that a temporal score appended to an
appearance ranking need not improve it. Temporal-bias benchmarks and analyses similarly show that
video models can rely on static evidence or fail to expose encoded temporal information under a
particular prompt or readout~\cite{lei2022singleframe,tempcompass,temporaltransfer,tracingaot}.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Conditional maneuver discrimination and cross-intersection retrieval measure different capabilities. The
sequence comparators can separate left from right turns at a known intersection yet provide too little
location discrimination to rank the evaluation gallery well. The top-1 decomposition localizes
the observed loss to wrong-intersection retrieval; neither a fixed reranking stage nor a
cross-validated linear blend of the two scores repairs this here. In parallel, multimodal LLMs can hold recoverable
temporal evidence that standard prompts and pooled readouts fail to surface. Recovering global
temporal retrieval therefore requires a temporal representation that is itself
location-discriminative, and an interface that exposes order rather than pooling it away---not
merely a better rule over existing scores or a larger model.

\textbf{Deployment and limitations.} BoT yields one descriptor per clip and supports indexed
nearest-neighbor search; evaluating the retained gallery with DTW is not directly indexable and is
quadratic in sequence length. The cheaper BoT-first encoder-sequence cascade bounds DTW work but reduces accuracy here, so temporal
comparison adds cost without a measured retrieval gain. The retrieval study covers two driving
datasets, their $50$ largest mixed-direction clusters, one backbone, and one linear fusion family
on a $0.05$ grid. The MLLM study uses
conditional pair and reversal probes, not global MLLM retrieval, and some VLM results are
transcribed from experiment artifacts not included in the compact result bundle. These results
establish replicated and interface-dependent failure cases, not that temporal retrieval or nonlinear
fusion cannot succeed. We release the diagnostics to support broader tests.
