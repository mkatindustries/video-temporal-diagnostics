\begin{abstract}
Scalable video retrieval favors global descriptors that pool frame features and are
insensitive to motion direction: two traversals of the same road in opposite directions, or
left versus right turns at the same intersection, can receive similar scores. We ask whether
comparators that \emph{detect conditional maneuver discrimination}---telling such cases apart
\emph{within} a known location---also improve end-to-end retrieval over a full gallery. Using an
executable diagnostic suite (temporal scramble gradient; directed full-gallery retrieval;
leave-one-cluster-out linear score fusion), we show a consistent reversal on two driving datasets.
Per-frame V-JEPA~2 encoder-sequence dynamic time warping (DTW) clearly beats a bag-of-tokens (BoT)
cosine baseline on conditional pooled-pair discrimination (Honda HDD $0.942$ vs.\ $0.825$ AP;
nuScenes $0.867$ vs.\ $0.791$), yet \emph{underperforms} it on global query-wise retrieval
(HDD $0.177$ vs.\ $0.256$ mAP; nuScenes $0.141$ vs.\ $0.315$). A BoT$\to$DTW cascade lowers AP at
every evaluated $k$, and a leakage-safe held-out linear fusion shows no detected gain over BoT on
HDD ($+0.001$, $95\%$ CI $[-0.003, 0.004]$; selected weight $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$) and collapses
exactly onto BoT on nuScenes ($\alpha^\star{=}1.0$ in all folds). Temporal sensitivity is thus
necessary for some motion queries but, for these datasets, scores, and a linear fusion rule, not
sufficient for retrieval; the temporal signal must be made location-discriminative rather than
appended to an appearance ranking. We release the diagnostics and evaluation protocol.
\keywords{Video retrieval \and Temporal sensitivity \and Evaluation}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Video-retrieval systems that must scale to large galleries typically represent a clip by a single
pooled descriptor and rank by cosine similarity. Such descriptors are, by construction, largely
insensitive to the \emph{order} of frames: reversing a clip or swapping a left turn for a right
turn at the same intersection changes little in a mean-pooled embedding. A natural remedy is to
compare per-frame feature \emph{sequences} with an order-aware comparator such as dynamic time
warping (DTW)~\cite{sakoe1978dtw}. On conditional discrimination tasks---distinguishing maneuvers \emph{within} a fixed
location---such comparators are markedly stronger than pooled baselines.

This paper asks the follow-up question that determines whether that strength matters in practice:
\emph{does conditional temporal discrimination transfer to global, query-wise retrieval?} We
evaluate it directly with a directed full-gallery protocol and a leakage-safe held-out fusion of
the appearance and temporal scores. Across Honda HDD and nuScenes we find a clean reversal: the
order-aware comparator that wins conditionally \emph{loses} globally, a two-stage cascade degrades
accuracy at every operating point, and a cross-validated linear fusion assigns essentially all
weight to the appearance score. The contribution is an evaluation result and protocol, deliberately
scoped: it concerns these two driving datasets, the specific representation/comparator pairs we
test, and a linear fusion rule---not a claim about video models in general.

\section{Diagnostics and Protocols}
\label{sec:protocols}
\textbf{Representations and comparators.} We compare pooled, order-invariant baselines---bag of
frames (BoF), Chamfer matching, and a V-JEPA~2~\cite{vjepa2} bag-of-tokens (BoT) cosine
descriptor---against order-aware sequence comparators applied to per-frame features: V-JEPA~2
encoder-sequence DTW and a temporal-residual DTW, and a DINOv3~\cite{dinov3} attention-trajectory
variant; a symmetric temporal max-similarity comparator~\cite{kordopatis2019visil} is a further
sequence baseline. Pooled descriptors of independently encoded frames are exactly permutation invariant;
contextual encoders are only empirically near-invariant, which the diagnostics expose.

\textbf{Order-sensitivity diagnostic (motivation).} We scramble a clip into $K$ near-equal chunks
($K\!\in\!\{1,\dots,16\}$) and shuffle them, averaging over ten seeds. On VCDB~\cite{vcdb}
(\cref{fig:scramble}) order-aware representations and comparators respond to scrambling while pooled
baselines remain flat, confirming the comparators carry genuine order information before any
retrieval question is asked.

\textbf{Directed full-gallery retrieval.} Each evaluation segment is a query; every other segment
is a gallery candidate. A candidate is relevant if it shares the query's GPS-clustered intersection
\emph{and} maneuver label; out-of-cluster candidates remain negatives. We report query-macro mAP
and MRR with intersection-cluster bootstrap confidence intervals. This is distinct from the
\emph{conditional pooled-pair} AP, which scores same-cluster pairs only.

\textbf{Cascade and held-out fusion.} We test (i) a BoT$\to$DTW cascade that reranks the BoT top-$k$
by DTW, and (ii) a per-query linear fusion $\alpha\,z(\text{BoT}) + (1{-}\alpha)\,z(-\text{DTW})$ of
$z$-scored scores. The weight $\alpha$ is chosen by leave-one-cluster-out cross-validation: for each
held-out intersection cluster, $\alpha$ is tuned on the other clusters with the held-out cluster
removed from both tuning queries and their galleries, then applied to the held-out queries against
the full gallery. This prevents the evaluated queries from informing their own weight.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
On Honda HDD~\cite{honda_hdd} the evaluation uses $1{,}687$ maneuver segments in $50$ mixed GPS
clusters; on nuScenes~\cite{nuscenes} (v1.0-trainval) it uses $264$ segments from $50$ clusters, with $222$ eligible queries drawn from
$40$ clusters. All numbers are query-macro means with $95\%$ intersection-cluster bootstrap
intervals.

\textbf{Conditional discrimination favors the sequence comparator.} As summarized in
\cref{tab:reversal} (top), encoder-sequence DTW attains the highest conditional pooled-pair AP on
both datasets, and the paired cluster-bootstrap difference over BoT excludes zero on both
(HDD $+0.117$, $[0.044, 0.127]$; nuScenes $+0.075$, $[0.028, 0.128]$).

\textbf{The ranking reverses in global retrieval.} On the directed full-gallery protocol
(\cref{tab:reversal}, middle) the order is inverted: BoT reaches mAP $0.256$ on HDD and $0.315$ on
nuScenes, while encoder-sequence DTW reaches only $0.177$ and $0.141$. On nuScenes the paired global
difference encoder-sequence DTW $-$ BoT is $-0.175$ $[-0.245, -0.117]$; on HDD the marginal
intervals likewise place DTW below BoT. Intuitively, DTW has no notion of location and promotes
temporally similar segments from the wrong intersection once out-of-cluster negatives are restored.

\textbf{Neither cascade nor held-out fusion recovers the gap.} Reranking the BoT top-$k$ by DTW
lowers AP at every evaluated $k$ (HDD: $0.127\!\to\!0.109$ at $k{=}10$ through
$0.223\!\to\!0.156$ at $k{=}250$; nuScenes: $0.255\!\to\!0.185$ at $k{=}5$ through
$0.309\!\to\!0.141$ at $k{=}100$), on the same candidate set (recall is unchanged). The held-out
linear fusion (\cref{tab:reversal}, bottom) selects $\alpha^\star{=}0.95$ in all $50$ HDD folds and
$\alpha^\star{=}1.0$ in all $40$ nuScenes folds: on HDD the fused mAP shows no detected gain over
BoT ($+0.001$, $[-0.003, 0.004]$), and on nuScenes the fusion collapses exactly onto BoT (fused
$\equiv$ BoT, difference $0$). We report bootstrap difference intervals and the fraction of
resamples favoring one method; we make no significance-test claim.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Global video descriptors and pooled matching underpin scalable near-duplicate and event
retrieval~\cite{faiss}, while sequence-alignment comparators such as DTW and temporal
max-similarity~\cite{kordopatis2019visil} capture fine-grained order. Recent analysis also bounds
what any fixed-dimensional embedding ranking can express~\cite{weller2025limits}, consistent with
our finding that a temporal score appended to an appearance ranking need not improve it. Prior analyses of temporal understanding in video models largely evaluate conditional or
classification settings; our contribution is to place a strong conditional comparator inside an
end-to-end retrieval protocol on the same data and observe that its advantage does not survive the
full gallery. We build on public driving datasets (Honda HDD, nuScenes) and open-weight video
encoders, and reuse a cluster-bootstrap uncertainty model appropriate to spatially grouped queries.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
Conditional maneuver discrimination and global retrieval measure different capabilities. A
comparator can separate left from right turns at a known intersection yet, lacking any location
signal, rank the whole gallery worse than an appearance descriptor; a fixed reranking stage or a
cross-validated linear blend of the two scores does not repair this, because the temporal score
carries no information about \emph{where}. Recovering global retrieval therefore requires a temporal
representation that is itself location-discriminative, not a better rule over existing appearance
and temporal scores. These conclusions are scoped to the two driving datasets, the specific
representations and comparators evaluated, and a linear fusion protocol; broader claims would
require additional domains, comparators, and fusion families. We release the diagnostics and the
directed-retrieval/fusion evaluation to support such follow-up.
